# Emergent Ventures Proposal Draft

## Tweet-length description

Built a bilingual evidence portal for Nepal's health sector with my own money while doing a PhD. 47M-observation dataset, 4-person team, MVP live. Need funding to register as NGO and scale to 150+ papers.

## Proposal (1,500 words max)

I grew up in a small town in Nepal where my family's experience with the national health insurance programme shaped my career. I watched health insurance change how my family and neighbours accessed care. I also watched it create problems nobody was measuring. People used services they did not need because they were suddenly free. The local health post was overwhelmed. Nobody in government seemed to have data on whether this was happening at scale, or what to do about it.

That observation brought me to a PhD in Economics at the University of Strathclyde in Scotland, where I study Nepal's National Health Insurance Programme using 47 million administrative observations. My research uses difference-in-differences and regression discontinuity designs to measure what happens when people gain insurance coverage. The early findings confirm what I saw at home: insurance improves access, but moral hazard is real, and the programme design does not account for it.

I am 27. Pursuing a PhD this early in life is uncommon where I come from. I funded the first stages of this work myself, spent a year and a half lobbying Nepal's government for the administrative data, and had to restart the lobbying process multiple times because the head of the Health Insurance Board changed three times during my PhD. I regularly woke at 5am to catch the start of office hours in Kathmandu for online meetings. Each change in leadership meant rebuilding trust from scratch.

Alongside research, I teach. As a Teaching Fellow at the University of Strathclyde, I run a Health Policy and Health Economics module for about 18 early-career professionals. I co-founded a reading group with Reardon Economics and EA Kathmandu University that reaches roughly 60 students in Nepal, working through health economics and causal inference papers.

All of this pointed me toward a problem that no amount of academic publishing would solve.

Comparative advantage works. Free trade improves welfare not because every country can produce everything, but because specialisation and exchange make everyone better off. I believe this is one of the most well-established results in economics, and I think the instinct to protect domestic production at the cost of consumer welfare is almost always wrong.

This idea shapes how I think about evidence for health policy. Nepal does not need to run its own randomised controlled trial on every health financing question. Thailand tested capitation payment reform. Rwanda tested performance-based financing. Indonesia tested a national insurance expansion remarkably similar to Nepal's. The evidence exists. What Nepal lacks is the infrastructure to find that evidence and adapt it. The comparative advantage of a small country with limited research funding is not in producing all its own evidence. It is in learning from others and applying those lessons to its own context. That is what NHPL is designed to do.

Nepal's health policymakers make decisions that affect 30 million people with almost no access to structured, relevant evidence. There is plenty of health research in the world, and a reasonable amount produced about Nepal. But none of it reaches the people who set policy, in a format they can use, in a language they read.

The Ministry of Health and Population relies on whatever evidence their donor-funded technical advisors bring them. WHO produces reports filtered through global frameworks. USAID-funded consultancies produce research driven by donor terms of reference, not Nepal's priorities. When the project ends, the evidence disappears. Academic papers sit behind paywalls in English-language journals that no Joint Secretary at the Ministry has time or access to read.

Nobody maintains a standing, publicly searchable, bilingual repository of health evidence systematically contextualised for Nepal's policy environment. This is the gap.

I co-founded the Nepal Health Policy Lab to fill it. The website is live at nepalhealthpolicylab.org. We built it with our own money and time. It is a bilingual (English and Nepali) evidence portal that takes global health research and translates each paper into a structured one-page summary: What Was Studied, What They Found, What This Means for Nepal. A health official in a rural municipality who reads only Nepali can access the same evidence as a WHO consultant in Geneva.

The site currently has a working evidence portal with seed papers, fuzzy search, filters by policy domain and study design, and a Build-a-Brief tool that lets users select a topic and comparison countries to generate a custom evidence report with PDF export. We have approval from the Nepal Health Research Council. Our team of four includes a PhD health economist (me), a PhD health policy researcher at UNC Chapel Hill, a programme affiliate in Kathmandu, and a staff member at Nepal's Health Insurance Board who provides insider access to implementation data and policy questions.

We are not a consultancy responding to donor contracts. We are an independent, Nepali-led evidence institution that sets its own research questions based on what Nepal's health system actually needs. We plan to publish original working papers alongside curated global evidence, so policymakers get both "here is what the global evidence says" and "here is what our own analysis of Nepal's data shows." No one else in Nepal's health policy ecosystem offers this combination.

The institutional turnover I experienced during my PhD, two changes at the head of the Department of Health Services and three at the Health Insurance Board in three years, taught me something important. Personal relationships with government officials are fragile. An institution that outlasts any individual appointment is not. That is why I decided to build something concrete: a platform and an organisation that would carry research and relationships forward regardless of who sits in which chair.

This grant would fund three things.

NGO registration in Nepal. Without it, we cannot sign institutional agreements, receive grants from other funders, or engage government formally. Our Kathmandu-based team member is ready to file.

Scaling the evidence portal from seed papers to 150+ by moving to a dynamic, AI-assisted architecture. The site is currently static. With API access to language models, we can build a pipeline that ingests new papers, generates draft summaries and Nepal contextualisations, flags them for human verification, and publishes. The Build-a-Brief tool improves substantially: instead of assembling pre-written summaries, it synthesises across dozens of papers in real time, generating briefs tailored to a user's specific policy question.

One policy engagement trip to Kathmandu to meet the Ministry of Health, the Health Insurance Board, and two potential institutional partners. Anchoring relationships to an institution rather than a person is the entire point.
