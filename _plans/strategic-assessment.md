# Strategic Assessment: Nepal Health Policy Lab

**Date**: 2026-03-09
**Perspective**: LMIC health systems expert with Nepal health landscape experience
**Purpose**: Honest gap analysis, strengths/weaknesses, and actionable recommendations

## 1. Gap Analysis: Is This a Genuine Gap?

**The short answer: Yes, but it is narrower than the proposal implies, and the positioning needs sharpening.**

Nepal's health policy ecosystem has several established actors doing adjacent work:

- **Nepal Health Research Council (NHRC)** -- the government's own health research regulatory body. Publishes the *Journal of Nepal Health Research Council*, maintains an ethical review function, and is the official clearinghouse for health research. NHRC does not, however, do what NHPL proposes: systematic curation and contextualisation of *global* evidence for Nepali policymakers. NHRC's focus is on domestic research governance, not evidence translation.

- **HERD International** -- the most direct competitor. A Kathmandu-based health research and development consultancy that does health systems research, policy analysis, and donor-funded evaluations. HERD produces policy briefs and has deep government relationships. However, HERD is consultancy-driven (it responds to donor ToRs), not a standing evidence translation service. It does not maintain a searchable public evidence database.

- **Nick Simons Institute (NSI)** -- focused on rural health training and workforce development, particularly in mid-western Nepal. Not an evidence synthesis organisation.

- **Health Research and Social Development Forum (HERD/F)** -- distinct from HERD International, this is a smaller outfit focused on operational research. Limited public output.

- **Nepal Public Health Foundation (NPHF)** -- positioned as a policy think tank but has been relatively quiet in recent years. Does not maintain an evidence portal or systematic translation function.

- **Policy Research Institute (PRI)** -- government-linked, covers all sectors. Health is a small part of its portfolio. Lacks health-specific depth.

- **International actors** -- WHO Nepal, UNICEF, World Bank, USAID-funded projects (such as the former NHSSP run by Options Consultancy) all produce evidence and policy briefs, but these are project-bound, donor-driven, and typically disappear when funding cycles end. They do not provide a standing, independent, publicly accessible evidence infrastructure.

**The genuine gap**: Nobody in Nepal currently maintains a standing, publicly searchable, bilingual database of global health evidence systematically contextualised for Nepal's policy environment. The "translation layer" -- taking a Thai UHC paper or a Rwandan PBF evaluation and explaining what it means for Nepal specifically -- is genuinely underserved. Policymakers at MoHP, if they engage with evidence at all, rely on whatever their donor-funded technical advisors bring them, or on whatever comes up in WHO/World Bank consultations.

**Where the gap claim is overstated**: The proposal sometimes reads as though no one is doing health policy research in Nepal. In fact, there is a substantial research community -- the problem is not the absence of research but the absence of an accessible, structured, demand-driven synthesis function. The proposal should be more precise about what it is and is not.

### The Original Research Gap -- Where NHPL Can Excel

Beyond evidence curation, there is an even larger and more consequential gap: **the near-total absence of independent, Nepal-specific original health policy research produced outside donor project cycles.** This deserves emphasis because it represents NHPL's greatest opportunity for differentiation and impact.

Consider the current landscape:

- **HERD International** produces research, but it is commissioned by donors against specific Terms of Reference. The research questions are set by USAID, FCDO, or the World Bank -- not by Nepal's own policy needs. When the project ends, the research programme ends.
- **NHRC** governs research ethics and publishes a journal, but it does not conduct independent policy analysis. Its journal publishes whatever is submitted, mostly clinical and epidemiological studies, not the kind of health systems and policy research that shapes government decisions.
- **Academic institutions** (IoM, BPKIHS, Patan Academy) produce student dissertations and faculty publications, but these are driven by academic incentive structures (journal impact factors, university promotion criteria), not by what the MoHP Secretary needs on their desk next month. The gap between what gets published in Nepali academic journals and what is policy-actionable is vast.
- **International actors** (WHO, World Bank) produce high-quality technical reports, but these reflect global institutional priorities and frameworks. A WHO report on Nepal's health financing will be filtered through WHO's UHC framework. An independent Nepali analysis of the same topic might reach different, more contextually honest conclusions.

**What is missing is an independent voice that asks its own research questions based on Nepal's actual policy needs, produces rigorous analysis unconstrained by donor agendas or academic publication incentives, and publishes it in accessible formats designed for the people who make decisions.**

This is where NHPL's team composition becomes a strategic asset, not just a nice-to-have:
- Sabin's PhD on Nepal's health insurance programme is *exactly* this kind of work -- original, Nepal-specific, policy-relevant health economics research. This should not be buried in a journal article that MoHP officials will never read. It should be the flagship product.
- Mukesh's health policy expertise can produce the kind of systems-level analysis (how federalism is reshaping health service delivery, what the BHSP implementation data actually shows) that nobody else is doing independently.
- Suresh's position inside HIB provides access to implementation data and policy questions that external researchers cannot see.

**The strategic implication**: NHPL should position itself not just as an evidence *curator* but as an evidence *producer*. The evidence portal (curating global research) is the foundation. Original research is the differentiator. The combination -- "here is what the global evidence says, and here is what our own analysis of Nepal's data shows" -- is something no one else in the ecosystem offers.

**Recommendation: Add a Working Papers section to the website.** This would:
- Provide a home for original NHPL research that is more formal than a blog post but faster and more accessible than a journal article
- Follow the model used by NBER, CGD, the World Bank, and other policy research institutions -- numbered working papers with a consistent format, citable with a DOI or persistent URL
- Allow the team to publish dissertation findings, policy analyses, and data-driven briefs on their own timeline, without waiting for journal peer review (which can take 12-18 months)
- Create a body of citable work that builds institutional credibility -- "NHPL Working Paper No. 7" carries more weight than a blog post
- Attract contributions from other Nepali health researchers (PhD students, early-career faculty) who want a fast, credible, Nepal-focused outlet for policy-relevant work
- Signal to donors and government that NHPL is a research institution, not just a content aggregator

The working paper format should be: abstract, key policy messages (1 page), full analysis, references. Bilingual abstracts and key policy messages, with the full paper in whichever language the author writes in. This mirrors the evidence portal's one-pager structure and maintains the "accessibility first" philosophy.

## 2. Strengths

1. **The "Nepal translation" layer is the killer feature.** The one-pager format with "What Was Studied / What They Found / What This Means for Nepal" is exactly right. A Joint Secretary at MoHP does not need to read a 30-page Health Policy and Planning article. They need a one-page brief that says: "Thailand did X at your income level, it worked because of Y, and here's what you'd need to adapt." This format delivers that.

2. **Health-only focus is strategically wise.** Nepal has too many generalist "policy institutes" that cover everything from governance to education to climate. Going deep on health, where the founders have genuine domain expertise (health economics PhD, health policy PhD, HIB insider), is the right call.

3. **The technical execution is impressive for the stage.** A working bilingual static site with Preact islands, fuzzy search, a Build-a-Brief tool with PDF export, validated data schemas, and a clear multi-agent pipeline plan -- all built before any funding -- demonstrates serious commitment and technical capability.

4. **Bilingual by design, not as an afterthought.** The Nepali translations of evidence contextualisations are genuinely useful. A Health Section Chief at a rural municipality in Baglung is not reading English-language journals. If the Nepali content is high-quality, this is a significant differentiator.

5. **The Build-a-Brief tool is a smart product.** Self-service evidence briefs that a WHO country office staffer or an INGO programme manager can generate without commissioning a consultant is a compelling value proposition. With more papers in the database, this becomes genuinely useful.

6. **Named authors on blog posts** signals academic seriousness and creates incentives for contributors. This is how you attract PhD students and early-career researchers who need publications and visibility.

7. **The team composition has strategic value.** Having someone inside the Health Insurance Board (Suresh Tinkari) and a field coordinator in Kathmandu (Prajjwal) alongside two diaspora PhD researchers is a good mix -- if the relationships are actually leveraged.

## 3. Weaknesses and Risks

### A. Political Economy Naivety

The proposal's framing around the March 2026 elections and the "window of opportunity" with a new government is concerning. The assumption that a new government creates a 12-18 month window for evidence-based policy influence reflects an outsider's theory of change that rarely holds in Nepal's political reality:

- Nepal's health bureaucracy (MoHP's career civil servants at the Joint Secretary and Division Chief level) is far more influential on day-to-day health policy than elected politicians. Ministers change frequently; Secretaries and Joint Secretaries run the system. **The target audience is the bureaucracy, not the politicians.**
- The Rastriya Swatantra Party's capacity to govern is untested. Their health policy positions are vague. A new party with limited governance experience may be *less* receptive to outside evidence than an established government.
- Health policy in Nepal is heavily shaped by donor priorities. USAID, FCDO, WHO, and the World Bank have more influence on the MoHP's agenda than any domestic think tank. NHPL needs to understand this power dynamic and position accordingly -- either as a complement to donor TA or as an independent voice that can say things donors cannot.

### B. Credibility Deficit

This is the biggest risk. Two diaspora PhD students without institutional backing, no publications record in Nepal's health policy space (yet), and no formal organisational registration will struggle to be taken seriously by:

- MoHP officials who are accustomed to dealing with WHO, World Bank, and established Nepali research organisations
- Donors who have their own trusted TA providers
- The Nepali academic community, which can be territorial

The university affiliations (Strathclyde, UNC) help somewhat, but they are not Nepal-based institutions. In Nepal's health policy world, institutional credibility is earned through presence, relationships, and sustained engagement -- not through a website.

### C. Content Quality at Scale

The 10 seed papers are well-chosen and the contextualisations are thoughtful. But:

- The AI-assisted pipeline is not yet built. The plan to scale to hundreds of papers quickly through AI parsing is ambitious. The quality control challenge is significant. A single poorly contextualised paper -- one that gets Nepal's health system structure wrong, or makes a naive recommendation -- could damage credibility irreparably.
- The Nepali translations need review by native Nepali speakers with health policy vocabulary. Machine or AI translation of health economics terminology into Nepali is treacherous. Terms like "capitation," "adverse selection," "quasi-experimental" need careful handling.

### D. The "10 Papers" Problem

The site currently has 10 papers. For a Joint Secretary or a WHO staff member landing on this site, 10 papers is not a database -- it is a reading list. The evidence portal's value proposition depends on volume and coverage. Below about 100 papers across the five topic areas, the portal does not justify the infrastructure. The Build-a-Brief tool currently generates briefs from 2-4 papers per topic -- that is not a brief, it is a bibliography.

### E. Sustainability Model is Undefined

The funding pathway section of the proposal is the weakest part. "We will discuss the funding strategy as a team" is not a strategy. Specific risks:

- ESRC IAA grants are small (typically 5,000-15,000 GBP) and short-term
- Commissioned reports from donors require an established reputation and procurement eligibility
- The "free for public, paid for institutions" model requires a user base that does not yet exist

### F. Missing Topics

The current five topics miss several of Nepal's most pressing health policy challenges:

- **Non-communicable diseases (NCDs)**: Nepal is undergoing an epidemiological transition. NCDs now account for 66% of deaths. Hypertension, diabetes, COPD, and cardiovascular disease are overwhelming a system built for infectious disease and maternal care. This is arguably the most urgent evidence gap.
- **Mental health**: Nepal has a severe mental health treatment gap (>90%). The Mental Health Act is recent and implementation is nascent.
- **Climate and health**: Nepal is one of the most climate-vulnerable countries globally. Health impacts of climate change are policy-relevant and evidence-rich.
- **Digital health / health information systems**: DHIS2 implementation, telemedicine, electronic medical records -- active policy discussions.
- **Health workforce migration**: Nepal exports health workers at a staggering rate. Evidence on retention, bonding schemes, and rural deployment incentives is directly relevant.

## 4. Content Strategy Recommendations

### What would make the portal genuinely useful:

**For a Joint Secretary at MoHP:**
- Evidence briefs on topics currently on their desk: NHIP enrollment expansion, BHSP costing, local government health planning capacity, Essential Drug List procurement reform
- Comparative evidence from countries Nepal's bureaucracy benchmarks against: Thailand, Sri Lanka, Bangladesh, Rwanda
- Policy options framed as choices with evidence for each, not as recommendations. Bureaucrats want options with evidence strength ratings, not to be told what to do.

**For a WHO Country Office staffer:**
- Evidence on WHO Nepal Country Cooperation Strategy priorities
- Cross-country evidence on whatever technical area they support
- The Build-a-Brief tool with 50+ papers per topic

**For INGO programme managers:**
- Evidence on specific interventions they are implementing
- Cost-effectiveness evidence for programme design

**Priority topics for the first 100 papers (in order of policy salience):**
1. Health financing and NHIP (Sabin's dissertation topic -- leverage this)
2. Federalism and health governance (the defining structural challenge)
3. NCD prevention and management in primary care (the emerging crisis)
4. Community health workforce (FCHV professionalisation is an active debate)
5. Mental health service delivery (policy window is open with the new Act)

## Summary Verdict

The Nepal Health Policy Lab addresses a real gap -- the absence of a standing, bilingual, publicly accessible evidence translation service for Nepal's health sector. The technical execution is strong, the health-only focus is strategically sound, and the one-pager format with Nepal contextualisation is the right product.

The main risks are: premature launch before sufficient content volume, lack of institutional credibility in Nepal, an underdeveloped sustainability model, and a theory of change that overestimates political windows and underestimates bureaucratic relationships and donor ecosystem dynamics.

**The path forward**: Build volume fast (50+ papers), publish original research (NHIP dissertation), secure one institutional partnership, launch the Digest newsletter, and build 3-5 genuine MoHP relationships. Do these five things in 90 days and the initiative has a real chance. Skip them and it risks remaining a well-built website that nobody in Nepal's health policy community uses.
